Form
First Act - Dialogue:
Mondō-2 → Ageuta-2
Synopsis
Munechika is told to have faith and forge the requested sword.
Text
Poetry in regular rhythm.
Music
Chant set in hiranori, delivered by the jiutai in yowagin style, accompanied by the hayashi. The percussion is in strict, and the nohkan in non-congruent rhythmic setting.
Visuals
This shite performs some basic kata and a modest opening of the stage space around Square 1.

The Dialogue dan concludes with a congruent chant that increases the Intermedia Index. The jiutai speaks for the young boy, as it delivers the chant. Its poetic text carries the boy’s words of encouragements for Munechika. It is set in hiranori, accompanied by the hand-percussion instruments in strict rhythmic setting and a non-congruent nohkan's part. The dramatic expression is very much heightened by the use of formal kata that align well with the musical changes but not the boy’s words. Against his words of encouragement, the movements of the two characters appear to display the mistrust and tension between them.

Munechika is clearly uneasy about meeting an unfamiliar boy who knows so much about him. He is not reassured when the boy says, "Walls have ears: In a world where rocks may speak…". The distrust between the two characters becomes clear when they break eye contact and turn away from each other. This happens shortly before the uchikiri, which separates the ageuta’s first line of text from its repetition. The powerful kakegoe ‘iya’ and the nohkan’s hishigi takane mi kusari pattern, energize the music and carry the melodic line that follows the repetition into the higher range, emphasizing the corresponding words. In this case, the word "kakure", which means ‘hidden’, maintains the feeling of distrust when the two protagonists continue to keep their distance from each other.

Upon hearing a reference to the "radiance of the Emperor’s sword," the two characters turn towards each another, attempting one more time to establish trust. The shite even goes as far as to take three steps towards the waki, but the effort is in vain, and ultimately fails when the shite turns away from the waki and circles to Square 1. The nohkan underlines the tense situation with the entrance of the naka no takane pattern.

Finally, after rebuilding momentum with a Forward Pointing and an Open-retreat, the two protagonists once again turn towards each other, and as the jiutai intones the words "[The sword] must be realized," the shite takes One Step towards the waki, suggesting that the communication line between the two is now open.

Although subtle, the changes in the music structure follow the unfolding narrative. The three attempts to establish trust between the boy and the swordsmith are matched with three changes in the delivery mode, from the declamatory mitsuji-utai, used to set up the attempt, to the rhythmic tsuzuke-utai used for the first two unsuccessful tries and the last propitious one:

#1a – Mitsuji-utai
In a world where rocks may speak,
#1b – Tsuzuke-utai
Nothing is hidden.
#2a – Mitsuji-utai
…Nothing shall dim the radiance…
#2b – Tsuzuke-utai
…of the Emperor’s sword.
#3a – Mitsuji-utai
Only have faith…
#3b – Tsuzuke-utai
through his Majesty’s grace and sword…

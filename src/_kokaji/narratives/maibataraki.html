Form
Second Act - Shite Performs:
Noriji-2 → Maibataraki → Noriji-3
Synopsis
The deity shows off its power through dance.
Text
None.
Music
All three percussion instruments in strict and the nohkan in congruent rhythmic settings.
Visuals
Short, dynamic instrumental dance using most of the stage space. The waki faces the back of the stage, preparing for the following scene of blade-forging by lifting the cloak from his right arm.

The maibataraki has the highest Index of Intermedia in the play. It is a fast and energetic dance accompanied by congruent nohkan with the three percussion instruments in strict rhythm. Whereas the kuse and kiri are dance-to-text, the maibataraki is an instrumental dance based primarily on abstract yet expressive kata. The word "maibataraki " is a combination of mai, signifying pure dance, and hataraki, referring to mimetic dances from the early days of Noh. In Kokaji’s case, it can be understood as a scene in which the deity descends from the heavens and shows off his power before taking part in the sword forging.

Musically, the dance has a three-section structure: kakari, ji, and dan. The dance structure loosely matches the musical one, with the kakari serving as a stamping introduction, ji for opening the stage-space and overtaking its powerful center, and dan providing closure and return toward the back of the stage.

Please note that because of great complexity and limited space on the page the percussion score shows only the taiko part.

Kakari

The kakari, abbreviated in the score as 'Ka,' is two-honji long and matches the introductory stamping of the shite. The word "kakari " can be translated as ‘taking the time to perform something,’ or 'initiating something.' In the case of the maibataraki, the kakari establishes the dance’s dynamic and upbeat mood. The shite customarily performs stamps at the beginning of a dance, as if calling the listener to attention. The dance opens with a sequence of Six Stamps, each one inphase with one of the percussionists’ strokes. The first one is also in phase with the very first note of the nohkan, which plays in a congruent setting for the second and last time in the play.

Ji

The form of this section, abbreviated in the score as 'Ji,' is musically led by the nohkan’s patterns, while the dance initiates an opening of the stage-space, taking the shite to the strong center-stage position. The nohkan introduces its core sequence of three patterns that we labelled in the score as a, b, and c, each lasting one honji. The sequence is played twice. It opens and closes with the distinguishable kashira head-patterns of the taiko.

Against this temporal framework the shite traverses first the right lateral (Squares 1 to 3), then the front stage (Square 3 to 5), and finally the left-right transversal (Square 5 to 8). This trajectory not only opens up the stage-space, it also creates a spatial wave of tension-release. The backstage positions of Squares 1 and 8, as positions of rest, are often used as starting and closing positions for a dance and its various sections. On the other hand, front-stage positions of Squares 3, 4, 5 and the center-stage Square 9, offer strength and energy. Therefore, in the sequence the motion Square 1 to 3 builds momentum, Square 3 to 5 maintains it, and Square 5 to 8 releases tension. It is interesting to note that this sequence of expansion of the stage-space in the maibataraki is identical to Hashitomi’s jonomai’s second section.

Dan

The temporal boundaries of this four-honji closing section, abbreviated in the score as 'Cl,' are articulated by the hayashi. The nohkan initiates this with two new one-honji patterns (which we labelled 'd', and 'e' in the score), followed by a return of the pattern ‘a’ and the cadential pattern tome. The dan’s four honji also coincide with the taiko’s second cycle, its duration once again delineated by the kashira patterns. Finally, the taiko’s dropping out in the dan’s first honji creates a sudden change of density that helps distinguish its beginning.

During the dan, the shite occupies center-stage where he performs a series of impressive kata that reinforce his image as a powerful being. The sequence starts with Two Large Steps and a Jump Kneel, followed by a Double-Kneel, and a Flip Left Sleeve. This is followed with a Right Circlet and a Circle back to Square 1, where he performs an Open-retreat, a kata often used as a closing gesture.

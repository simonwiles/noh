Form
Second Act - Waki Waits:
Notto → Noriji-1
Synopsis
Munechika performs a purifying ritual and prays for divine help in carrying out the imperial order.
Text
Poetry in irregular rhythm.
Music
The jiutai sings in tsuyogin style a text set in ōnori with the accompaniment of all three percussion instruments in strict rhythmic setting.
Visuals
The waki kneels on the dais at Square 4 and performs purifying gestures with a wand.

The Intermedia Index rises as the music of the noriji 1 becomes congruent with the poetic text, set in ōnori, and the accompaniment of the three percussion instruments in strict rhythmic setting. The intermedia interest of the noriji 1 lies in how it raises the energy and creates a transition between the adjacent shōdan.

At first, the noriji-1 is visually static. Munechika kneels on the dais motionless as he announces that he is going to pray. The jiutai delivers the prayer, the density increasing in comparison to the single voice in the preceding notto. A moment later, Munechika begins to wave a sacred wand as the jiutai speaks for him, calling for divine help. The music builds momentum with its faster tempo and larger instrumentation, as the taiko has now joined the two hand-percussion instruments.

The noriji 1 leads to the hayafue, the entrance music that will bring on stage the second-act shite, personifying a divine being. The text and tempo artfully provide the prayer with a tone of intense reverence. Set in ōnori, the poetic text counts on average eight syllables per honji. In preparation for the swordsmith’s statement of entreaty, “hear and grant my prayer”, the tempo slows down when the taiko pattern switches from two to one stroke per beat. This leads to the dramatic line “kinjo-saihai”, indicating a prayer. At this point, the text setting switches to non-congruent and slows to three syllables per honji. The waki sings the first word, the jiutai the second. Against the slow-down in the vocal line, the percussion instruments create an accelerando that sets the tone for the dynamic hayafue and the entrance of the second-act shite.

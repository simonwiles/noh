--- title: Hashitomi ---

Synopsis

First act: A monk, performing a ritual celebrating flowers, notices with bewilderment a white one that seems to smile. He asks its name. A woman's voice identifies it as a yūgao, an ‘evening flower.’

Interlude: A local villager in a conversation with the monk tells the story of Lady Yūgao. We learn that passing through Gojō on his way to visit his mistress Lady Rokujō, Prince Genji admired the abundant yūgao flowers growing on a dwelling. He asked his retainer, Koremitsu, to pick some. The blossoms came on a scented fan with a poem written on it by a beautiful woman, later referred to as Lady Yūgao. Genji replied with his own, leading to a romantic tryst. The relationship was short-lived, though, as Lady Yūgao became possessed by a vengeful spirit.

Second Act: The monk visits the house of Lady Yūgao in Gojō. Her spirit appears and reminisces about her first night with Genji, their introduction through yūgao flowers, and the poems they wrote to each other on a fan.

Background

Setting
First Act: End of summer at dusk, at Unrin-in Temple in Murasakino, Kyoto.
Second Act: At night, in front of the house of Lady Yūgao in Gojō, Kyoto.
Tsukurimono
Second Act: A hinged lattice shutter set on Square 8 represents Lady Yūgao’s house.
Characters

Intermedia Form

The Shōdan Map under the video represents the play’s intermedia form. It also shows that the play is composed of two acts, the first one comprised of eight shōdan, and the second act comprised of nine.

The background color grouping blocks shows that most shōdan are bound into dan. Act I has four dan: Waki Enters, Shite Enters, Dialogue, and Shite Exits; Act II has five: Waki Waits, Shite Re-enters, Dialogue, Shite Performs, and Shite Exits. Bringing the cursor over a shōdan identifies its name and the dan to which it belongs.

A comparison between the duration of the two acts shows that the first one is about half the duration of the second, suggesting that the setting-up of the plot in the first act is simpler and less elaborate than the developments in the second act.

The height of the blocks shows the development of the play’s Index of Intermedia (IoI). The different heights demonstrate that the development is not linear but comes in waves. We have identified three kinds of waves:

First: the rising wave within a dan. In the first act, the IoI of each dan forms a wave leading to a peak on its last shōdan. For example, the First act’s Waki Enters is composed on three shōdan, whose index increases exponentially from the first to the third. The development of the IoI over the two shōdan of the Shite Enters is similar. Things are different though, with the remaining two dan. The Dialogue, composed of only one shōdan, is followed by the Shite Exits, which has two. In this case, it is over their combined three shōdan that the IoI increases exponentially.

Second: the rising wave across an act. There is an important difference in the way the IoI of each act transforms. The first act contains three waves. The first two correspond to the entrance of the waki and shite, respectively. The third one corresponds to the setting of the play’s premise. On the other hand, there is only one wave in the second act, as the IoI of subsequent dan keeps increasing until the instrumental dance in the Shite Performs dan. These waves suggest a gradual but hesitant setting-up of the plot in the first act, followed by a continuous and steady development in the second one.

Third: the rising wave of the play as a whole. The IoI is at its lowest at the beginning of Act I, while its three highest peaks create a wave that culminates on the third one. Because the play’s premise is already set, the IoI at the beginning of Act II is higher than it was at the beginning of Act I, but lower than the first act’s peak. But it does not take long for the IoI to exceed the first act’s peak when reaching the highest IoI with the instrumental dance.

Act I

Waki Enters

Composed of three shōdan, the dan sets up the scene for the main character to appear. As it is usually the case, this role is assigned to the secondary actor, the waki.

As expected, the dan’s overall Intermedia Index is low. It remains non-congruent throughout the expansion of its textures. However, the index’s progression over its three shōdan displays a gradual increase in intermedia complexity. The resulting rise of momentum helps prepare the stage for the upcoming entrance of the shite.

The dan opens with Nanoribue, an unmetered entrance music played by the nohkan. It accompanies the waki as he walks towards the stage. The melody’s free-flowing and calm tone creates a serene ambiance, fitting for the entrance of a Buddhist monk.

Upon reaching Square 1, one of stage’s most restful positions, the waki delivers the Nanori, an unaccompanied monologue , introducing himself in prose.

The dan is concluded by Kakaru 1, a shōdan with the richest texture so far. It mixes spoken and chanted text , brings in the two percussion instruments for the first time, and brings the waki to center of the stage.

Shite Enters

The dan’s function is to introduce the protagonist. However, the shite interrupts his entrance at the First Pine, though, delaying his entry onto the stage-proper for a later dan. Composed of only two shōdan, it is non-congruent throughout. The overall Intermedia Index is low since this all occurs in the character-introduction phase of the play.

Like the preceding dan, this one also opens with a non-congruent entrance music , the Ashirai. This time it is performed by the nohkan accompanied by the two percussion instruments. This is the first shōdan involving the full hayashi.

While the waki introduces himself in spoken prose, the shite uses poetry, performing the Song in unmetered sashinori chant. This raises the Intermedia Index in preparation for the next dan, the dialogue.

Dialogue

The Dialogue section consists of a single shōdan. Its function is to set the play’s premise through an exchange between the two actors. As it is often the case,this dan portrays the waki's curiosity about the shite’s identity.

The overall Intermedia Index stays low. The shōdan returns to spoken prose but includes also some chanting in unmetered sashinori, and engages both shite and waki.

The dan is also significant dramatically. The shite not only enters the stage-proper, but also establishes contact with the waki.

Formally, the dan appears to be a preparation for the next one: the Shite Exits. The Intermedia Index shows an energetic curve starting with the Dialogue, which climaxes with the Ageuta 1, in the Shite Exits dan.

Shite Performs

There is no Shite Performs dan in Hashitomi’s first act. The shōdan that traditionally defines this dan – the Kuse – is included in the second act.

Shite Exits

The dan concludes the setting of the play's premise and prepares for its development in Act II, before the shite leaves the stage. The Intermedia Index slope shows that the dan maintains the momentum from the preceding one before reaching the peak of Act I. It then drops dramatically to a silent and slow exit.

The dan consists of two shōdan. As a pair, the two shōdan could be compared to the rongi, a shōdan commonly used to close Act I. They follow the same structure: the first part here, the Kakaru 2,is a dialogue partially spoken and partially sung in non-congruent setting, followed by a poetic and congruent chant, here, the Ageuta 1.

The Kakaru 2 flows freely as a non-congruent chant with text in prose, accompanied by the percussion instruments in flexible setting. It is delivered as the two protagonists remain essentially motionless in their respective positions. The shōdan concludes with a clue about the shite’s identity. The Kakaru 2 refers to a mansion whose location is then identified at the very beginning of the next shōdan.

This important moment in the dramatic unfolding of the play is underlined by the Ageuta 1 , which introduces the first congruent chant in the play and the first entrance of the jiutai. Moreover, in preparation for his exit, and for the first time in the play, the shite performs a sequence of kata.

Interlude

Fitting with its typical function and form, the kyōgenInterlude in Hashitomi features an un-masked kyōgen actor as a villager in dialogue with the monk. For most of the shōdan they kneel on the floor and remain almost motionless. We hear a 'plain-spoken' story of Lady Yūgao, which gives the essential background of the play's main character.


Act II

Waki Waits

The Waki Waits dan restarts the narration and sets the groundwork for the shite to re-enter. In Hashitomi, the dan is composed of a single shōdan. Descriptive prose switches to poetry at the conclusion. It is a non-congruent sashinori chant for one voice, accompanied by the two hand-percussion instruments in flexible rhythmic setting, and performed almost without motion. The overall Intermedia Index stays in the lower range.

Intermedia analysis:
Kakaru 3

Shite Re-enters

The function of the Shite Re-enters dan is to re-introduce the shite, now, in his new guise. The Kongō school’s utaibon describes the first act shite as a woman, the second act one as the woman’s spirit, the costume change indicating the difference between the two.

The dan is composed of three shōdan, and brings the first definitive move towards congruency, raising the Intermedia Index, which will peak with the instrumental dance. Although the first shōdan in the sequence is still non-congruent, it expands the overall texture with an einori chant for one voice and jiutai, accompanied by a non-congruent nohkan’s part and the two hand-percussion instruments already in strict rhythmic setting. Two congruent chants follow it, both set in hiranori accompanied by the two hand-percussion instruments still in strict rhythmic setting. The shite sings the first one, and the jiutai sings the second one.

The rapid development of the dan’s musical texture counterbalances the absence of any dance movements. However, the staging increases the visual impact in two steps. First, the shite’s entrance is strictly aural, since he sings the issei-chant from inside the tsukurimono draped in a violet cloth, thus remaining invisible. This creates great mystery and builds anticipation. Finally, about half-way in the ageuta, the spirit is made visible when the drape around the tsukurimono is slowly removed. However, this time the spirit is mute, as the jiutai delivers the chant.

Ageuta 2

Dialogue

While the Dialogue dan in Act I established the play’s premise, this dan introduces the climax of the play. It creates a context and reason for the shite to dance.

The dan consists of a single shōdan, the rongi. It is a congruent sung dialogue performed by the shite and the jiutai. Its poetic text is set in hiranori, accompanied by the full hayashi with the percussion in strict setting, and a non-congruent nohkan. Set for two voices instead of one, unlike preceding two chants, this shōdan continues to raise the Intermedia Index. Although the performance of kata is rather limited – the shite remains seated for almost the entire time – in this shōdan, the shite walks out from behind the lattice, the hashitomi, and asserts his presence on the stage.

Intermedia analysis:
Rongi

Shite Performs

The play's Intermedia Index peaks in this dan. It is composed of two shōdan, each one a highlight in its own right.

It starts with the Kuse, the first dance-to-text shōdan of the play. Its poetic text set in hiranori is illustrated with various mimetic kata. It is accompanied by the two hand-percussion instruments in strict rhythmic setting, and a non-congruent nohkan part.

Next, the Jonomai, is a congruent instrumental dance , in which the two-hand percussion instruments maintain their strict rhythmic setting and, for the first and only time in the play, we hear a congruent nohkan part. Following a shōdan that directly correlates text and mimetic dance, this one features pure dance in its full poetic beauty.

Jonomai

Shite Exits

The final dan consists of two shōdan. The first, the Waka, is a momentary interuption from congruency that will return with the final shōdan, the Kiri, which concludes the play with the second highest Intermedia Index.

The absence of dance and nohkan in the Waka, both of which carried the jonomai for the last fifteen minutes, is noticeable. The singing returns to non-congruent chant. However, the shite maintains sufficient tension, delivering the poetic text standing in the central Square 9, accompanied of the two hand-percussion in strict rhythm.

The Kiri brings back movement with a dance-to-text shōdan, that uses mainly the strongest stage positions, the central square 9 and the front of the stage. The jiutai and shite sing the poetic text, accompanied by the two hand-percussion instruments in strict rhythmic setting. In the final moments, the nohkan returns with a closing pattern. The intermedia spectacle ends at full force and then dissolves when the audience sees the shite disappear behind the hashitomi, as the music slows down dramatically.
